Singapore Hub Launch Marks Major Step in Asia-Pacific Strategy

The 50,000 sq ft facility will serve as regional headquarters, with plans to hire 200 employees by end of 2025. The expansion signals DataBao's ambitious growth plans in the world's fastest-growing technology market.

ML
Michelle Liu Asia-Pacific Bureau Chief
December 22, 2024 5 min read

SINGAPORE — In the gleaming towers of Marina Bay, where the world's biggest technology and financial firms jostle for talent and market share, a new player is staking its claim.

DataBao, the enterprise analytics company that has become a darling of Silicon Valley, officially opened its Asia-Pacific headquarters here on Monday, marking its most significant international expansion to date.

The 50,000-square-foot facility occupies three floors of the Marina One complex, with views stretching across the Singapore Strait. By the end of next year, the company plans to employ 200 people here, a mix of engineers, salespeople, and customer success managers focused on serving the region's rapidly growing enterprise technology market.

A Bet on Asia's Digital Transformation

"This isn't just about having a presence in Asia," said Michael Torres, DataBao's chief executive, who flew in from San Francisco for the opening ceremony. "This is about building a true center of excellence that can serve customers across the region."

The investment comes as Asia-Pacific emerges as the fastest-growing market for enterprise software. According to IDC, spending on cloud and data analytics in the region is expected to grow 18 percent annually through 2028, outpacing growth in North America and Europe.

"The enterprises here are not laggards. Many are leapfrogging legacy systems entirely and going straight to cloud-native, AI-first architectures." — Michael Torres, CEO

Singapore was chosen over other regional hubs like Hong Kong and Tokyo for a combination of factors: its English-speaking workforce, its robust legal and financial infrastructure, and its strategic location at the crossroads of Southeast Asia's major economies.

Aggressive Hiring Plans

The company has already hired its first 40 Singapore-based employees and is actively recruiting for roles ranging from machine learning engineers to enterprise account executives. Unlike some technology companies that use Asian offices primarily for sales and support, DataBao plans to conduct significant research and development work in Singapore.

"We're not just building a sales outpost," said Priya Sharma, DataBao's chief technology officer. "Some of our most innovative work on real-time analytics is going to happen right here."

"Singapore gives us access to talent from across Asia—engineers from India, product managers from Australia, salespeople who know the local markets."

Jennifer Walsh, Chief Revenue Officer

The company is offering relocation packages to experienced hires from its California headquarters and has partnered with several Singapore universities to create an internship pipeline.

Key Customer Wins

The expansion is not purely speculative. DataBao already has significant enterprise customers in the region, including three of Singapore's largest banks and several major telecommunications providers in Southeast Asia.

During the opening ceremony, the company announced new partnerships with DBS Bank, one of Asia's largest financial institutions, and Singapore Airlines. Both companies will deploy DataBao's platform across their data analytics operations.

"What attracted us to DataBao was the platform's ability to unify data from hundreds of sources in real time," said a senior technology executive at DBS who spoke on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak publicly. "For a bank our size, that capability is transformative."

Government Support

The Singapore Economic Development Board, the government agency that courts foreign investment, played an active role in wooing DataBao. While neither side would disclose specifics, government incentives for technology companies setting up regional headquarters in Singapore can include tax breaks, grants for research and development, and subsidized training programs.

"We see data analytics and A.I. as critical enabling technologies," said an EDB spokesperson. "Companies like DataBao help strengthen Singapore's position as a global technology hub."

Competition Intensifies

DataBao is not the only Western technology company expanding its Asian footprint. Snowflake, Databricks, and several other data analytics firms have all significantly increased their presence in the region over the past two years.

But Torres argued that the market is large enough to accommodate multiple winners. "Enterprise data analytics is still in its early innings in Asia," he said. "The question isn't whether we'll compete with each other for market share—it's whether we can collectively help enterprises here unlock the value in their data."

The Singapore office is expected to be fully operational by the end of Q1 2025, with additional satellite offices planned for Sydney, Tokyo, and Mumbai in the following 18 months.

For DataBao, which went public last year to considerable fanfare, the Asian expansion represents the next chapter in its transformation from a Silicon Valley startup into a global enterprise software company. Whether it can execute on that ambition may depend heavily on what happens in this gleaming tower overlooking Singapore harbor.
